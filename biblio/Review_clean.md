## Behavioral/cognitive description of autism: some "modeling theories"

### Executive functions

* deficit in executive functions  
  * concept formation, cognitive flexibility, fluency, action planning, response inhibition, and working memory  

---

### Focus on details / integration / complexity

* weak central coherence (WCC): cognitive style focused on details → reduced global processing or contextual integration  
  * implicit language: requires integration of multiple information sources + context  
  * social: difficulty understanding social situations globally (over-focus on specific elements, e.g., one word)  
  * limited interests / attraction to regularities  
  * initial version: inability to integrate information  
  * later version: local processing bias rather than a true deficit  

* Baron-Cohen (2006): hyper-systemizing theory of autism  
  * systemizing mechanism set too high in autistic individuals  
  * difficulty coping with high-variance systems  
  * parallels with WCC but framed as adaptive: detail focus serves system understanding  

* monotropism (Murray, 2005): narrow range of interests // related to WCC  
  * competition for attention shapes cognitive processes  
  * lack of generalization  
    * top-down processing: strict / limited / resistant to correction  
    * hyper-awareness within attentional focus ("attentional tunnel"), hypo-awareness outside  

* Dwyer (2024): supports monotropism account of ASD  
  * strong sustained attention on one subject, difficulty dividing attention  
  * atypical regulation of attention rather than a simple deficit  
  * ASD: both  
    * increased endogenous hyper-focus  
    * increased susceptibility to exogenous attentional capture (inattention)  
  * greater hyper-focus correlated with lower quality of life (QOL)  
    * but positive effects on QOL mediated by meaningful engagement in the focused activity  

* Ben-Yosef (2016)  
  * compares three accounts:  
    * weak central coherence (local vs global)  
    * complexity account  
    * social cognition deficit  
  * no deficit in processing human stimuli, nor in global processing (conceptual or perceptual)  
    * despite previous findings reporting deficits  
  * intact context processing in direct associative contexts (e.g., image + sound of a dog)  
  * impaired integrative context processing (no single cue directly leads to the target; requires integration)  
  * conclusion: difficulty when synchronization between brain regions is required, but not limited to this  
    * also general cognitive overload  
    * supports complexity account of ASD  

* Mottron (2000)  
  * cognitive differences observed across domains (e.g., visuospatial, music, language, memory) in ASD  
    * hypothesis: multi-modal deficit?  
      *  deficit across sensory modalities or across cognitive operations within a modality?
    * alternative (WCC): deficit in integration into higher-level organization → detail focus  
  * hierarchization deficit hypothesis (HDH):  
    * local and global processing intact  
    * but global processing has no advantage over local processing  
  * same/different judgments with:  
    * local change (note change)  
    * global change (transposition) in melodies  
  * findings:  
    * no impairment in global processing  
    * but local processing advantage  
  * interpretation:  
    * enhanced perception of elementary sound properties rather than integration deficit ?
  * Paradoxical Functional Facilitation:  
    * neurological deficit may induce enhanced performance  
    * preserved perceptive processes compensate for impaired non-perceptive ones

### Atypical sensory processing

* overreactivity as a primary symptom in autism (Liss, 2006)  
  * hypothesis: individuals with ASD experience over-arousal → leads to over-selective attention and repetitive behaviors  
  * cluster analysis: subgroup with both overreactivity and over-selective attention  
    * over-focusing group: highest cognitive functioning but most socially impaired  
      * over-focus detrimental to social behavior but not to intellectual functioning?  
    * lowest-functioning group: more under-reactive  
      * possibly linked to intellectual disability  
  * hypothesis: over-selective attention and repetitive behaviors as compensation mechanisms  
    * predictable/repetitive events regulate arousal levels  
  * over-arousal hypothesis reformulated as "noisy neuronal firing"  
    * Hussman (2001): reduced inhibition (GABAergic neurons)  
    * Casanova (2002): cortical minicolumns smaller and more numerous → overconnectivity, reduced selectivity  
    * Kinsbourne: exaggerated ascending activation from brainstem to cortex  

* Gomes (2008): hypersensitivity  
  * sensory abnormalities linked to attention  
  * high reactivity associated with increased attention, and vice versa  

* Hilton (2007): link between sensory and social profiles  
  * Dunn (1997) sensory processing model:  
    * low/high threshold × active/passive response  
  * moderate to strong relationship between sensory responses and social functioning  
  * strongest for:  
    * sensation avoiding (low threshold, active): avoidance of overwhelming social interactions?  
    * sensory sensitivity (low threshold, passive): distractibility / hyperactivity → missed social cues  
  * moderate for:  
    * sensation seeking (high threshold, active): atypical behaviors may discourage peers  
    * low registration (high threshold, passive): reduced detection of sensory input → missed social cues

## Empathy

* Milton (2012): double empathy problem  
  * non-autistic individuals may also lack understanding/empathy toward autistic individuals  

* Crompton (2020): “telephone game” paradigm  
  * autistic–autistic and non-autistic–non-autistic chains: effective communication  
  * mixed chains: reduced efficiency  
  * interpretation:  
    * communication difficulties arise from neurotype mismatch, not solely from deficits  
    * information transfer may be more effective within same neurotype  

* Alkhaldi (2021):  
  * neurotypical individuals report disliking autistic individuals more  
  * perceived as awkward and lacking empathy  

* Dunn (2023):  
  * autistic individuals rate themselves as awkward  
  * but still show willingness to engage socially  
  * suggests they are not universally perceived as “undesirable”  
  

### Other

* Female Autism Phenotype (FAP) (Hull, 2020)  
  * higher social motivation, but difficulty maintaining long-term relationships  
  * camouflaging: use of conscious or unconscious strategies (explicitly learned or implicitly developed) to minimize the appearance of autistic characteristics during social situations  
    * more difficult to diagnose, often misdiagnosed as anxiety, etc.  
    * strong associations between camouflaging and poor mental health outcomes

  * AgCC shows intermediate scores between control and ASD  
    * suggests only part of the explanation?  

* Wheelwright (2010): Autism Spectrum Quotient (AQ)  
  * NAP: narrow autism phenotype (>3 SD)  
  * BAP: broad autism phenotype (>1 SD)  
  * MAP: medium autism phenotype (>2 SD)  
  * higher prevalence:  
    * males > females  
    * scientists, especially mathematicians  
    * parents of autistic individuals

## Findings about neural correlates in ASD

* Piven (1997): smaller corpus callosum (CC), larger cortical regions (especially parietal)  
  * CC smaller only when controlling for brain size  
    * may reflect enlarged cortical regions rather than true CC reduction  
    * especially in regions without strong CC connections  

* Just (2006): fronto-parietal underconnectivity  
  * may arise from anatomical constraints (e.g., smaller genu of CC)  
  * Casanova (2002): more short-range fibers than long-range → deficit in long-distance connectivity  
  * executive dysfunction possibly linked to fronto-parietal underconnectivity  
  * normal activation within individual regions  
    * difficulties arise in tasks requiring integration across regions  
      * theory of mind, face processing, executive functions → all rely on integration  
      * postural deficits? (Minshew, 2004)  
  * conclusion:  
    * interest in studying functional connectivity in individuals with complete or partial agenesis of the corpus callosum (AgCC) with preserved cognition  
    * to understand how absence of CC affects interhemispheric coordination  

* Mason (2008):  
  * three types of inference:  
    * intentional (theory of mind-based)  
    * physical  
    * emotional  
  * ASD: increased right hemisphere (RH) activation during reading for all inference types and context sentences  
    * hypothesis: spillover effect → RH recruited due to left hemisphere (LH) overload  
  * reduced functional connectivity:  
    * within theory of mind network (left medial frontal gyrus <-> right temporo-parietal junction)  
    * between theory of mind network and LH language network (left inferior frontal gyrus, left superior temporal gyrus)  
  * functional connectivity (frontal–parietal) correlated with size of CC genu  

* Alexander (2007):  
  * only a subgroup of autistic individuals shows decreased fractional anisotropy (FA) / increased mean diffusivity  
    * driven by increased radial diffusivity  
  * suggests myelination deficit  
  * correlated with CC size and processing speed  
  * similar findings in Shukla (2010)  

* Belmonte (2004):  
  * ASD: high local connectivity + low long-range connectivity (cf. Just et al., 2004)  
  * overconnectivity in sensory areas:  
    * excessive activation (low selectivity)  
    * no clear distinction between target and non-target stimuli  
    * leads to overload at later processing stages  
    * integrative areas receive degraded input → reduced activation  

* Belmonte (2004): cerebellum  
  * implicated in long-range connectivity and coordination of cognitive functions  
  * ASD: smaller cerebellum, fewer Purkinje cells (inhibitory)  
    * high physical connectivity but low computational connectivity  
    * linked to cognitive, motor, and social deficits  

* Rubenstein (2003): modulated noise exposure model of hypersensitivity  
  * critical developmental window for establishing excitation/inhibition balance and sensory maps  
    * also influences closure of critical periods in cortex  
  * modulated noise: early closure → poor differentiation (low signal-to-noise ratio)  
  * continuous noise: no closure → absence of stabilization signal  
  * GABAergic neurons:  
    * lateral inhibition (between cortical columns)  
    * intra-column regulation (prevent excessive activation)  
    * timing of synaptic plasticity  

* Dapretto (2005): mirror neuron system (MNS)  
  * no MNS activation during observation/imitation of emotional expressions in ASD  
  * behavioral performance similar to controls (no difference in fixation duration on eyes/faces)  
  * suggests reliance on visual/motor attention rather than MNS  
  * MNS activity correlated with social ability  

* Diamond (2019): fixel-based analysis in ASD  
  * decreased fiber density (FD) in CC (splenium and genu) → reduced axonal density?  
  * decreased global white matter FD (GWM-FD), but smaller effect than in CC  
    * suggests more widespread (but milder) alterations  
  * negative correlation between social impairment and fiber density in CC splenium  

* Paul (2012) hypothesis:  
  * when the brain lacks callosal connections (e.g., AgCC), it develops alternative pathways  
    * to maintain bilateral resting-state networks (RSNs) and interhemispheric connectivity  
  * callosotomy data suggest:  
    * minor commissures and thalamocortical pathways may support compensation  

* Demopoulos (2015): conceptual model of “sensory processing inefficiency”  
  * hypothesis: impaired interhemispheric communication via CC contributes to inefficient information processing  
    * difficulty integrating relevant social information  
  * CC may support language learning and cortical development by:  
    * enabling rapid transfer of auditory and visual information  
    * toward dominant language hemisphere for integration and interpretation

### General model for hypotheses on autism (inspired by Rubenstein, 2003)

* 1. Early genetic or environmental alteration (gene mutation or atypical environment)  
* 2. Reduced number of GABAergic neurons in cortex/amygdala (reduced production or migration)  
* 3. Reduced inhibition (GABA) relative to excitation (glutamate) → excitation/inhibition imbalance  
* 4. Excessive plasticity (e.g., increased long-term potentiation, LTP)  
  * overly rapid learning, reduced flexibility  
  * increased reactivity to stimuli  
* 5. Underdifferentiated representations  
  * simple stimuli activate large neuronal populations  
* 6. Local hyperexcitability → reduced differentiation → decreased long-range excitability  
  * leads to reduced long-distance connectivity  
* 7. Emergence of atypical behaviors (e.g., hypersensitivity, rigid memory, reduced flexibility, increased epilepsy risk)  


## Neural correlates of AgCC

* anatomy (Tovar-Moll, 2006):  
  * some properties preserved, others strongly altered → compensatory or maladaptive?  
  * partial AgCC (pAgCC):  
    * hypothesis: genu (anterior CC) forms first, then progressively more posterior regions  
      * consistent with remnant CC mainly consisting of genu  
    * preserved topographic organization similar to typical CC  
      * remnant genu connects mainly frontal regions (no posterior expansion)  
      * general organization: frontal/genu, parietal/body, occipital/splenium  
  * Probst bundles (PB): ipsilateral bundles, not seen in controls or contralateral hemisphere → not an artifact  
    * present in majority of cases + preserved topographic organization → compensatory?  
  * sigmoid bundle (SB): S-shaped, connects right frontal lobe to left occipito-parietal regions  
    * present in ~30% of patients, often those most severely impaired → maladaptive?  
  * Lazarev (2016): PB and SB may support synchrony between heterotopic regions (EEG coherence)  

* functional connectivity (synchronization of signals)  
  * Hinkley (2012): reduced connectivity in some cortical regions (resting-state alpha)  
    * frontal, parietal, occipital regions → linked to altered cognitive functions in AgCC  
    * activity still present, but reduced connectivity  
    * correlated with executive function measures  

* structural connectivity (physical pathways)  
  * Shi (2021):  
    * reduced interhemispheric connectivity  
    * increased intrahemispheric connectivity  
    * structural alterations not strongly associated with broad neurobehavioral outcomes in children with AgCC  

* Owen (2013):  
  * qualitative organization of resting-state networks similar between controls and AgCC  
  * reduced interhemispheric connectivity in associative networks, not sensory networks  
    * thalamus may maintain symmetric bilateral connectivity (especially for sensory processing)  
  * Hett (2006): anterior commissure ~10% larger in AgCC  
    * correlated with better cognition (Barr, 2002)  
  * high variability across AgCC individuals  
    * multiple compensatory strategies → difficult to study  

* Yuan (2020):  
  * link between structural connectivity (SC) and functional connectivity (FC)  
    * faster-conducting fibers → stronger synchronization of neural signals  
  * increased intrahemispheric SC/FC → possible compensatory mechanism in AgCC  
  * compensation hypothesis: increased lateralization to compensate for reduced interhemispheric connectivity  
    * more left-handed in (small) population AgCC and right lateralization of left limbic area

* Szczupak (2020):  
  * AgCC alters interhemispheric synchrony of cortical communication  
  * follows developmental gradient:  
    * early-developing regions (antero-posterior, medio-lateral CC development) less affected  
  * posterior sensory regions (homotopic connections) more affected  
  * cingulate cortex (pioneer axons): preserved synchrony but delayed  

* myelinated CC axons connect homotopic regions and terminate in cortex  
  * modulate activation/inhibition (GABAergic neurons)  
  * regulate excitation/inhibition balance  
  * enable interhemispheric inhibition → control lateralization and synchronization

## Genetics

* Tick (2015): meta-analysis of twin studies in ASD  
  * monozygotic twins (MZ): correlation // 0.98  
  * dizygotic twins (DZ): correlation // 0.53  
  * heritability estimates: 64–91%  
  * extreme cases do not show higher heritability → supports continuum rather than distinct categories  

* Lau (2012):  
  * in AgCC: structural abnormalities often associated with de novo chromosomal mutations  
  * low recurrence risk in families → behavioral phenotypes may also be discontinuous (Sherr et al., 2005)  

* Lau (2012): shared ASD–AgCC genetic factors  
  * ARX gene mutations (affect connectivity and neuronal excitation)  
  * Schell (2003): ARX (Aristaless Related Homeobox)  
    * critical for brain and neuronal development  
    * associated with widespread central nervous system malformations  
    * linked to autism, AgCC, dystonia, epilepsy, thalamic dysplasia, West syndrome  
  * Schell (2005):  
    * often de novo mutations  
    * insertions/deletions identified via CGH array (comparative genomic hybridization)  
    * males more severely affected (single X chromosome)  
    * phenotype depends on gene dosage (e.g., deletion = 1 copy, duplication = 3 copies)  

* Belmonte (2004):  
  * CMH protein involved in synaptic pruning altered in ASD  
  * cerebellar alterations (Purkinje cells) → social deficits  

* Rodriguez (2021): three gene categories involved in ASD  
  * neurotransmitter receptors (e.g., serotonin, dopamine, GABA)  
  * genes involved in neurotransmitter metabolism (e.g., tryptophan → serotonin, melatonin)  
  * cell adhesion molecules and scaffolding proteins  

* Wahlsten (2003): mouse model of AgCC  
  * BTBR T/+ tf/tf strain: AgCC + reduced hippocampal commissure  
    * cross between heterozygous T gene and homozygous tufted mutation

## Role of the corpus callosum (CC)

* Banich (1998):  
  * CC enables transfer of sensory information and coordination between hemispheres  
  * role in attention: allows division of labor between hemispheres → increased computational capacity  
    * dynamic interhemispheric interaction increases processing capacity  
    * trade-off between computational gain and integration cost  
  * two hypotheses:  
    * “gating” mechanism when one hemisphere exceeds capacity  
    * CC as an attentional control system  
  * attention conceptualized as:  
    * resource  
    * selective gating mechanism  

* Yuan (2020):  
  * increased intrahemispheric connectivity → compensatory in AgCC  
  * possible increased lateralization  

* Hinkley (2016):  
  * pAgCC: bilateral language representation  
  * complete AgCC (cAgCC): right-lateralized language  
  * reduced left lateralization linked to lower verbal IQ (VIQ) → clinical implications  
  * some non-language regions less activated in LH  
    * compensation may come at cost of reduced processing in these regions  
  * supports CC role: interhemispheric inhibition (suppress one hemisphere while the other is active)  

* Karolis (2019):  
  * interhemispheric independence hypothesis  
    * brain expansion → functional lateralization to reduce conduction delays  
    * lateralized regions less strongly connected via CC  
  * improves efficiency but reduces recovery after lesions  

* Gazzaniga (2000): evolutionary perspective  
  * lateralization = development of new skills, maintenance of others
  * new functions (e.g., language) emerge in one hemisphere  
  * other hemisphere maintains older functions  
    * lateralization → specialization + preservation  
  * non-lateralized processing allows parallel processing (requires CC coordination)  
  * CC enables specialization and coordination  
    * LH: language, problem-solving  
    * RH: face recognition, attention monitoring  
    * memory: LH (words), RH (faces), objects both hemispheres  
    * attention: endogenous (LH lateralized), exogenous (bilateral)  
  * split-brain findings:  
    * independent motor actions possible  
    * parallel simple tasks possible  
    * complex decisions require integration  
      * possibly subcortical coordination? 

* Monteiro (2019):  
  * lateralized activation (language, S1, S2) present in AgCC  
  * no symmetric activation → possible role of aberrant bundles  
  * lateralization alone does not explain absence of split-brain syndrome  

* Szczupak (2022):  
  * new techniques reveal sparse/low-density heterotopic connections → previously underestimated  
  * ~75% heterotopic connections in mouse brain  
    * mainly central CC core vs homotopic periphery  
  * more numerous in malformed brains (e.g., Probst bundles)  
    * previously considered atypical  
    * may be compensatory

## Description of CC agenesis

* Glass (2008):  
  * prevalence // 2/10,000  
  * frequent associated central nervous system malformations (49%)  
  * social impairments (often autism-like)  
  * 17% chromosomal abnormalities  
  * risk factors: maternal age, prematurity  

* Paul (2014): overlap between ASD and AgCC, especially in communication  

* Bedeschi (2006):  
  * wide variability in outcomes (from typical cognition to severe psychomotor delay)  
  * partial and complete AgCC: similar frequency of associated malformations, but different types  
    * partial AgCC → posterior fossa malformations  
    * complete AgCC → cortical development malformations  
  * frequent association with craniofacial abnormalities, genetic anomalies, epilepsy  

* Lau (2012):  
  * deficits in social skills, imagination, theory of mind, communication, attention switching  
  * no deficit in attention to detail  
  * intermediate scores between controls and ASD  
  * Badaruddin (2007): AgCC symptoms less severe than ASD  

* Paul (2014):  
  * difficulties in attention (mainly), social functioning, thought processes, somatic complaints  
  * no specific restricted interests or detail-focused attention

## Core syndrome (Brown, 2019)

### (1) Reduced interhemispheric transfer of sensory-motor information

* preserved integration for simple stimuli, impaired for complex tasks  
* deficit in bimanual coordination (Mueller, 2009; Gooijers, 2013)  
  * CC develops slowly + similar performance in children and AgCC → CC critical for bimanual coordination  
  * performance in complex asymmetric tasks improves with age (independent of simple motor skills)  
    * interhemispheric integration especially important for complex tasks  
* deficits in tactile-spatial processing (Dunn, 2000)  
* reduced visual and auditory memory (Paul, 2016)  
  * difficulty organizing new information for encoding (possible LH/RH integration issue)  
  * may also reflect general resource limitation (see point 3)  
* difficulty encoding and retrieving new information (Erickson, 2014)  
  * deficits in both initial learning and delayed recall  
  * HERA model:  
    * right prefrontal cortex → retrieval  
    * left prefrontal cortex → encoding  
  * commissurotomy: encoding and retrieval possible within each hemisphere  
    * weaker memory may reflect reduced integration  
  * interpretation:  
    * verbal memory deficits due to lack of integration between LH (serial/verbal) and RH (semantic/contextual) processing  

---

### (2) Reduced cognitive processing speed

* cognitive control differences mainly due to reduced processing speed (Marco, 2012)  
* deficits increase with task complexity (Mathias, 2004)  

---

### (3) Deficits in complex reasoning and novel problem-solving

* interhemispheric resources required for complex cognition (Koivisto, 2000)  
* impairments mainly in complex tasks (Schieffer, 2000)  
  * abstract reasoning (Brown, 2000)  
  * concept formation (Fischer, 1992)  
  * strategy formulation (Brown, 2012)  
  * generalization (Solursh, 1965)  
* no deficits in overlearned tasks (Erickson, 2013)  
* reduced creativity: coherent but low diversity outputs (Brown, 2024)  
  * creativity model: interhemispheric inhibition → diversity, then integration → coherence  
* difficulty integrating contextual information and generating hypotheses (Su, 2023)  
  * difficulty generating meaningful associations between words (Turk, 2010)  
    * possibly due to reduced LH/RH integration  
    * link with memory deficits (Paul, 2016)  

---

### (4) Difficulty integrating complex information → social difficulties (hypothesis)

* difficulties in emotional processing:  
  * difficulty identifying/describing emotions (Paul, 2021)  
    * but preserved emotional experience  
    * → deficit in cognitive processing of emotion  
  * impaired recognition of complex emotions (fear, anger) (Bridgman, 2014)  
  * difficulty expressing emotions verbally (Pazienza, 2011)  
  * reduced attention to eyes  
  * impaired face recognition (complex stimuli) (Paul, 2016)  
  * preserved basic emotion understanding but difficulty integrating in complex social contexts (Anderson, 2017)  
  * difficulty reasoning about emotions in context  
  * difficulty integrating cognitive and emotional information + anticipating consequences (Young, 2019)  
  * difficulty inferring others’ mental/emotional states (Kang, 2009)  
  * impaired judgment of social norms (Brown, 2021)  
  * deficits in emotional prosody and non-literal language (Paul, 2003)  
    * literal language preserved  
    * poor proverb/non-literal comprehension (Rehmel, 2016)  
    * emotional prosody requires both hemispheres  
    * reduced integration of RH (emotional/holistic) and LH (analytic) processing  
    * deficits stronger in complex tasks  

* difficulties in behavior:  
  * deficits in adaptive behavior, especially social skills (Haisley, 2025; Miller, 2024)  
  * rigid application of social norms (lack of contextual adaptation) (Brown, 2021)  
  * reduced flexibility (Marco, 2012)  
  * deficits in planning, organization, cognitive flexibility (Mangum, 2021)  
  * difficulty forming strategies, inconsistent decision-making, poor use of available information (Brown, 2012)  
  * under uncertainty: increased focus on potential loss (Brown, 2012)  
  * increased impulsivity / reduced inhibition

## Cognitive science in general 

### Discourse theory (Mason, 2006)

* dynamic recruitment of cortical networks depending on text constraints  

* previous discourse theories:  
  * coarse coding  
  * construction-integration framework  
  * salience hypothesis  

* three main hypotheses about the role of the right hemisphere (RH):  
  * spillover: recruitment of RH networks when left hemisphere (LH) capacity is exceeded  
  * RH involvement in inference integration  
  * coarse coding theory:  
    * inferences arise from activation of broadly coded semantic information in RH  

* parallel networks of discourse:

1. Basic LH sentence network (not specific to discourse)  
   * includes multiple LH regions  
   * functions: syntactic processing, word meaning, visual/graphemic processing, phonological and lexical-semantic processing  

2. Coarse semantic processing network (right middle and superior temporal regions)  
   * activates broad semantic representations and possible inferences  
   * detects overlap between activated meanings  

3. Coherence monitoring network (bilateral dorsolateral prefrontal cortex)  
   * increased activity when coherence is low or semantic field is unusual  
   * supports inference generation to restore coherence  

4. Text integration network (left inferior frontal + left anterior temporal regions)  
   * constructs a situation model / mental representation of the text  

5. Perspective-taking network (bilateral medial frontal + right posterior temporal/parietal regions)  
   * processes agent/protagonist perspective  
   * supports understanding of intentions, motivations, emotion-related memory  

6. Spatial imagery network (left-dominant, bilateral intraparietal sulcus)  
   * activated during high-imagery sentences or metaphors

### Four-level model for pathological speech

* (1) Sensorimotor control (linguistic-symbolic planning)  
  * regions: temporal-parietal, Broca’s area  
  * sound map: left ventral premotor cortex  

* (2) Speech-motor planning  
  * sequential organization of movements + co-articulation  
  * involves frontal cortical areas  
  * inverse models: generate motor commands  
  * forward models: predict consequences of motor commands  
    * impairment → difficulty initiating sounds, start–restart behavior  
  * regions (dominant hemisphere):  
    * prefrontal cortex  
    * area 6  
    * supplementary motor area (SMA)  
    * posterior parietal areas (5 and 7)  
    * Broca’s and Wernicke’s areas  

* (3) Speech-motor programming  
  * specification of muscle movements (timing, velocity) + prosody/emotional content  
    * atypical duration? 
  * rapid compensatory movements via auditory feedback  
  * circuits: sensorimotor cortex, lateral cerebellum, basal ganglia  

* (4) Speech execution  
  * transmission of motor commands to lower-level motor systems  

---

* Apraxia of speech (AOS):  
  * lesions in cortical motor planning areas  
    * possibly left ventral premotor cortex  
  * disruption of planning and/or programming (difficult to dissociate)  
  * symptoms:  
    * impaired prosody  
    * slow speech rate  
    * reduced articulation accuracy  
    * syllable-by-syllable speech

  * left ventral premotor cortex  
  * connectivity with SMA, Broca’s area  

* speech-motor planning:  
  * dominant hemisphere network: prefrontal cortex, area 6, SMA, posterior parietal (5, 7), Broca, Wernicke  

* speech-motor programming:  
  * cortical-subcortical circuits: sensorimotor cortex, lateral cerebellum, basal ganglia

    * difficulty with novel and complex tasks

  * more specific to ASD:

## Cognitive theories

* deficit in executive functions  
  * may result from fronto-parietal underconnectivity  

* weak central coherence: cognitive style biased toward details  
  * reduced global/contextual processing  
  * difficulty integrating multiple sources of information  

* complexity theory:  
  * deficit in integrative context processing  
  * preserved associative context processing  
  * leads to detail-focused processing  

* monotropism: restricted range of interests  
  * attentional competition  
  * hyperawareness within attentional focus, hypoawareness outside  

* hyper-systemizing theory:  
  * difficulty processing high-variance systems  
  * reliance on structured/systematic processing  
  * requires efficient integration

# Summary of key aspects of AgCC

* core syndrome (not independent components) (Brown, 2019):  

---

## (A) More specific to AgCC (less frequent in ASD)

### (1) Reduced interhemispheric transfer of sensory-motor information

* motor:  
  * preserved integration for simple stimuli  
  * impaired integration for complex tasks  
  * deficits in bimanual coordination and cross-modal tasks  

* memory:  
  * difficulty encoding verbal and visual information  
  * difficulty retrieving newly learned information (Erickson, 2014)  
  * possible interpretation:  
    * reduced ability to integrate RH semantic/contextual information with LH verbal memory  

---

### (2) Reduced cognitive processing speed

* cognitive control differences mainly driven by reduced processing speed (Marco, 2012)  
* deficits increase with task complexity (Mathias, 2004)  

---

## (B) Less specific to AgCC (largely shared with ASD)

### (3) Deficits in complex reasoning and novel problem-solving

* complex tasks:  
  * impaired in AgCC (Schieffer, 2000)  
  * rely on interhemispheric resources (Koivisto, 2000)  

* affected domains:  
  * abstract reasoning (Brown, 2000)  
  * concept formation (Fischer, 1992)  
  * strategy formulation (Brown, 2012)  
  * generalization (Solursh, 1965)  

* novel vs overlearned tasks:  
  * no deficit in overlearned processes (Erickson, 2013)  
  * interhemispheric contribution decreases with practice (Cherbuin, 2005)  

* creativity:  
  * difficulty generating stories/associations (Turk, 2010)  
  * outputs coherent but low diversity (Brown, 2024)  
  * possible explanation: reduced LH–RH integration  
  * creativity model: interhemispheric inhibition → diversity, then integration → coherence  

* reduced attention  
  * especially shifting

---

### (4) Hypothesis: difficulty integrating complex information → social deficits

* emotional processing:  
  * preserved emotional experience and coping (Paul, 2021)  
  * impaired emotional interpretation and cognitive processing:  
    * identifying emotions  
    * describing emotions  
    * integrating cognitive and emotional information  
    * reasoning in complex social contexts  
    * judging appropriate social norms  
  * impaired face recognition (complex stimuli) (Paul, 2016)  
  * difficulty inferring others’ mental/emotional states (Kang, 2009)  

* language:  
  * preserved literal language processing  
  * impaired emotional prosody and non-literal language (e.g., proverbs)  
    * emotional prosody requires both hemispheres  
    * reduced integration of RH (emotional/holistic) and LH (analytic) processing  
    * deficits increase with task complexity  

* behavior:  
  * reduced adaptive and flexible behavior  
    * rigid application of social norms (Brown, 2021)  
    * poor use of contextual information  
  * decision-making:  
    * difficulty using available information  
    * inconsistent strategies  
  * increased impulsivity / reduced inhibition  
  * reduced planning and organization  
  * no lack of social interest, but difficulty sustaining interaction

## Why ASD and AgCC share symptoms

* AgCC: absence of corpus callosum → interhemispheric disconnection  
* ASD: local hyperconnectivity → global underconnectivity  
* both involve connectivity alterations  
  * → difficulty integrating information across brain regions  

---

# Hypothesis about shared symptoms

* mainly affect:  
  * social cognition  
  * complex tasks (requiring integration)  

* core mechanism:  
  * impaired integration of information from multiple sources  

---

## Interhemispheric integration (LH–RH)

* LH: lexical, syntactic processing  
  * inferior frontal gyrus (IFG)  
  * superior and middle temporal gyri (STG, MTG)  

* RH: contextual and social processing  
  * superior temporal sulcus (STS): prosody, social cues, intentions  
  * middle temporal gyrus (MTG): coarse semantics, affective/social processing  
  * temporo-parietal junction (TPJ): perspective-taking, theory of mind  

* discourse theory (Mason, 2006):  
  * LH sentence network  
  * RH coarse semantic processing network  
  * LH text integration network  
  * bilateral perspective-taking network  

* hypothesis:  
  * reduced structural/functional integrity of fronto-parietal network  
    * (e.g., reduced size or connectivity)  
  * fronto-parietal network supports top-down integration across modalities and time  

---

## Multimodal integration deficit

* functional hypotheses:  
  * reduced connectivity between STS and sensory regions (visual/auditory)  
  * reduced connectivity between LH and RH hubs  

* structural hypotheses:  
  * reduced volume in integrative regions (TPJ, STS) (Par, 2021)  
  * DTI: reduced integrity of posterior CC and inferior longitudinal fasciculus  

---

## Complex task processing

* functional:  
  * reduced connectivity in fronto-parietal network  
    * affects reasoning, strategy, flexibility  

* structural:  
  * reduced integrity of anterior CC and superior longitudinal fasciculus (SLF)  
  * reduced volume in fronto-parietal regions:  
    * dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC)  
    * posterior parietal cortex (PPC)  

---

## Emotion / prosody / semantic integration

* functional:  
  * reduced connectivity between:  
    * RH temporal regions (STS, MTG, TPJ)  
    * LH frontal regions (IFG, STG, MTG)  
  * → reduced integration of emotional information into language  

* structural:  
  * reduced volume / fiber integrity in RH STS/MTG and bilateral amygdala  
  * reduced integrity of splenium  

---

* hypotheses to be extended

# ASD-specific (non-AgCC) hypotheses

* possibly linked to local hyperconnectivity  

* connectivity deficits in four networks (Elton, 2015):  
  * default mode network (DMN) → preserved in AgCC  
  * dorsal attention network (DAN)  
  * ventral attention network (VAN)  
  * executive control network (ECN / fronto-parietal)  

* monotropism (Dwyer, 2024):  
  * atypical attention regulation  
    * increased endogenous hyperfocus  
    * increased exogenous attentional capture  
  * DAN and VAN overactivation → reduced flexibility in ECN  
  * hypothesis: preserved in AgCC, altered in ASD  

---

## Hypersensitivity and attention

* ASD: strong link between hypersensitivity and attention  
  * possible mechanism:  
    * overselective attention and repetitive behaviors as regulation (Liss, 2006)  

* potential neural correlates:  
  * connectivity in DAN/VAN  
  * parietal cortex structure  

* hypothesis:  
  * hypersensitivity absent or weaker in AgCC  
  * weaker correlation with attention in AgCC  

---

## Cerebellum

* ASD:  
  * smaller cerebellum  
  * altered excitation/inhibition balance (reduced inhibition)  
  * abnormal cortico-cerebellar connectivity (Ramos, 2019)  
    * linked to social cognition  

* AgCC:  
  * not clearly affected? 
  * possibly increased cerebellar activation in resting-state (Siffredi, 2021)  
    * alternative compensatory pathway  

---

## Corpus callosum (ASD and partial AgCC)

* topographic organization:  
  * genu (frontal), body (parietal), splenium (occipital)  

* developmental hypothesis:  
  * anterior regions develop first (genu)  
    * consistent with remnant CC in partial AgCC  

* findings:  
  * fronto-parietal connectivity correlated with genu size  
  * social impairment negatively correlated with splenium fiber density  

* AgCC:  
  * fronto-parietal underconnectivity → executive deficits  
  * preserved activation within regions

* primary mechanism: disrupted interhemispheric communication
