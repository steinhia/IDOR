## Introduction 
* brain has to react to change
    * agentive change (goal directed) -> law to complex to systematize :'(
    * non-agentive change (precipitated event)
* change can be regular / fit patterns -> important to anticipate them to avoid negative impact
* systematizing to predict change
    * observation of input-operation-output -> search for structure in data -> formulating laws governing change
    * human behavior not lawful -> no bijection facial expression/thinking or response to events etc etc -> high variability
* ‘empathizing system’ : ‘hardware’ for dealing with the complex social world
  * Intentionality Detector (interprets everything as goal-directed)
  * Eye Direction Detector (automatically interprets eye-like stimuli as “looking at me” or “looking at something else”)
  * Shared Attention Mechanism (detects that 2 people are perceiving the same scene)
  * Theory of Mind Mechanism (representation of mental states)
  * Emotion Detector (represents affective states)
  * Empathizing SyStem (allows an empathic reaction to another's emotional state)
* 4 degrees of systemazing :
  * no interest to systematize -> can cope with any change
  * level 4 systemazise to much -> autistic traits / a lot of scientists / mathematicians
* hyper-systemizing theory -> searches for patterns everywhere or not
  * The systemizing mechanism (SM) drives the brain to look for input-operation-output relationships in any data, and to construct systems
  * everyone has a level (determined by biology) : not be able to deal with systems less than X% lawful
  * The higher the level of the SM, the less generalization
  * higher SM will be attracted to low-variance systems and attracted to details (help systematizing)
  * Asperger have higher level / parents of ASD are half-way there / classic autism higher SM than Asperger
  * results from 2 parents with high SM ?
* high level resulting symptoms :
  * repetitive behaviors / need for sameness / social withdrawal / reduced ability to generalize / immersion in details / narrow interests
* two dominant theories of the non-social features of autism 
  * the weak central coherence theory (Frith, 1989) 
    * attention to detail
    * results of clinical tests also ("mathematical" tasks) coherent with high systematization
  * the executive dysfunction theory (Russell, 1997)
    * perseveration when the rule changes / no cognitive flexibility
    * hypersystematizing / do not shift to a new rule ?
