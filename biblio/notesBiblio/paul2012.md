# How important is the corpus callosum in resting-state networks?
 
## Resume 

* typical bilateral resting-state networks can also emerge in the absence of callosal connections
 
## Introduction 
* Resting-state functional MRI (fMRI) examines spontaneous, low-frequency (<0.08 Hz) fluctuations in blood oxygenation level-dependent
(BOLD) signal in the absence of a specific task
* Temporal correlations between different brain regions in the resting-state BOLD (rsBOLD) signal are widely interpreted as an index of intrinsic functional brain connectivity at the neuronal level
* resting states are :
    * symmetric
    * homotopic regions have high correlations

## Callosotomy
* change of connectivity over time
    * 1 case just after
        * loose of synchro between homotopic regions
        * loss of connectivity depended on the regions : more pronounced in caudal regions
        * less loss of thalamus-cortex (preserved same side, small diminution other side) than cortex-cortex
        * network cortex-homotopic thalamus - other thalamus - other cortex as a compensation ?
    * 5 months after, 40 years after
        * relatively preserved connectivity
        * thalamo-cortical less affected than cortico-cortical
    * Hyp:
        * When the callosum is severed, the brain has a strong drive to re-establish bilateral RSNs and does so over time
        * The anterior comissure may facilitate interhemispheric connectivity of the rostral temporal cortex
        * Although attenuated following callosotomy, interhemispheric thalamocortical connectivity is less markedly impacted than corti- cal–cortical connectivity and may play an important role in the re-establishment of bilateral RSNs

## Discussion 
* thalamus = sensoriel/moteur, coordonne circulation infos dans le cerveau
* hypotheses
    * The corpus callosum is heavily involved in interhemispheric functional connectivity in intact brains; 
    * When a brain loses or never develops callosal connections, it develops alternative routes to maintain bilateral RSNs and interhemispheric functional connections
    * Data from callosotomy patients suggest that minor commissures and thalamocortical pathways may play a key role in this adaptation.
