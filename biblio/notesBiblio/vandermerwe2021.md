## Introduction 
* Apraxia of Speech (AoS)
  * increasedduration of pauses, abnormal prosodia
* 2 pre-execution phases (motor planning, programming), execution phase
* traditionally, linguistics, motor programming, execution -> aphasia, apraxia, dysarthria
* apraxia of speech would be a motor planning or programming deficit ?
* speech production models 
    * high level linguistic processor + controls prosody
    * planner (sequence of movements)
    * controller (speech plan and issue motor commands)
    * plant (vocal tract and articulators)
* forward internal model : can predict consequence of action 
* Feedback-aware control of tasks in speech (FACTS) model -> organize for a task, not reach articulatory movements
* DIVA model to explain AoS
  * feedforward controller + somatosensory feedback and auditory feedback
  * AoS more a feedforward deficit
  * highest level of processing : activation of the sound map (neural repr of sound, syll, even freq word)
    * sound map : left ventral premotor cortex
        * damage = deficit feedforward (sensory targets damaged)
    * direct connections to articulatory maps in primary motor cortex + via cortico-cerebellar loop
        * damage to these connections = deficit in feedforward loop (cannot access motor commands) -> impaired articulation
* motor planning 
  * internal models of the goal of action (motor representations)
  * cortical areas in the dominant hemisphere : prefrontal cortex, Area 6, supplementary motor areas, 5 and 7 (posterior parietal), Broca and Wernicke
* motor programming
  * bilateral subcortical areas
* lower levels in the motor system
* parrell2019 proposes 
  * motor planning : higher level cognitive goal -> transformation into motor commands
  * motor programming : mid-level motor hierarchy (transition between planning and execution) -> preparation of motor programs in basal ganglia and lateral cerebellum
  * planning/programming // strategy/tactics -> general plan vs particular specification in space and time
  * execution : motor cortex via thalamus, lower motor neurons

* neuro
  * speech (overt/covert) activated frontal motor areas
  
* Speech motor planning : interface between phono planning (selection of sound) and motor commands
  * speech acquisition : development of core motor plan (CMP) for each sound
    * inverse model (motor plan) : intentions (sensory output) to motor commands -> cerebellum ?
  * forward model predicts future state of the system and predicts sensory feedback
  * internal models : determines motor commands required to perform a task and predict consequences of motor commands
    * need adaptation (phonetic conetext, coarticulation) : frontal cortical areas
  * forward models : predict consequences of movement/future state -> posterior parietal cortex ?
  * sensory-motor integration : sylvian parietal-temporal ?
* Speech motor programming : motor plans -> muscle-specific programs (velocity, force etc)
  * sensorimotor cortex, lateral cerebellum, basal ganglia
  * suprasegmental features at this level!! prosody, stress, pauses, rythm, emotion! controlled by fondamental freq, amplitude and duration
  * basal ganglia disorder could lead to temporal inaccuracy in speech :silences, fast rate etc
  * basal ganglia (programming and execution) : motor control, but also cognition, emotional-motivational
  * often execution disorder with programmig disorder : primary motor cortex implicated in both programming and execution

* A four-level model for the characterization of pathological speech
  * sensorimotor control : linguistic-symbolic planning
    * temporal-parietal, Broca
  * speech-motor planning : sequential organization of movements for each sound + co-articulation
    * learning sensorimotor transformations during acquisition
    * inverse models generate motor commands
    * forward model predicts consequences of the motor commands
  * speech motor programming : specification of muscle's movements, velocity etc + suprasegmental/emotional information
    * rapid compensatory speech movements / auditory feedback
  * speech execution : descending instructions to the lowest levels of control
*  The  nature of pathophysiology may  include, for example, actual  damage  to  an inverse model  of a sound,  deficient retrieval  of  motor commands, inaccurate specification,  slow processing  of an efference  copy and  reduced range  of forward prediction and planning.
